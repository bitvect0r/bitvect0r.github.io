# The Impossibility of Universal Approval

## The Setup

People feel bad when someone doesn't like something about them. A coworker finds you too quiet. A friend thinks your hobby is pointless. A parent wishes you'd chosen a different career. The emotional response is almost automatic — a tightening, a desire to fix it, a low hum of shame.

But is that response rational? Underneath the feeling is an implicit expectation: *this thing about me should be likable to everyone.* Let's examine whether that expectation can survive contact with logic.

## The Core Argument

**Premise 1: No trait, object, quality, or characteristic exists that is universally liked by all people.**

This is an empirical claim, but an overwhelming one. Consider the strongest candidates for universally liked things:

- *Food and water when hungry/thirsty* — ascetics and hunger strikers deliberately refuse these.
- *Music* — some people are genuinely amusic and find it irritating.
- *Being loved* — people with avoidant attachment actively push it away.
- *Kindness* — some interpret it as weakness or manipulation.

If even the most basic human pleasures and virtues can't achieve universal approval, nothing can. The dataset of all humans is too large, too varied, too contradictory. For any X, there exists at least one person who dislikes X. This isn't a flaw in the world — it's a structural feature of how preferences work across a diverse population.

**Premise 2: If universal approval for any trait is impossible, then expecting it for a trait of yours is expecting something impossible.**

This follows directly. Your sense of humor, your appearance, your values, your voice — none of these are exempt from the rule. There is no version of you that everyone will like, because "universally liked" doesn't exist as a category.

**Conclusion: Feeling bad because someone doesn't like something about you is an emotional response to an impossible expectation going unmet.**

You are not reacting to a failure. You are reacting to a mathematical certainty as if it were a personal indictment.

## The "But This Person Matters" Objection

The logical argument above addresses the generalized anxiety — the background hum of "what if people don't like me." But real insecurity often has a face. It's not "someone out there disapproves," it's "my father disapproves" or "the person I love disapproves."

Does the argument still hold when the disapprover is someone important to you?

Yes. Whoever matters to you is still a member of the dataset of all people. However important they are to you — however much weight their opinion carries emotionally — they remain a single person with a single set of preferences, subject to the same structural reality: no one likes everything. Expecting someone to like everything about you, no matter how close they are to you, is expecting them to be a person who doesn't exist. The person who likes everything about everyone is a logical impossibility, because preferences require contrast. A mind that approves of everything has no preferences at all.

This doesn't mean their opinion is worthless. It means their disapproval of a specific trait is *expected behavior* from any human, not evidence that something is wrong with you.

## The Stronger Argument: Dislikability as Character

Here's where it goes from defensive to positive.

If universal likability were achievable, what would it require? You would need to sand off every edge, smooth every opinion, mute every strong preference. You would need to be agreeable to everyone — which means never challenging anyone, never standing for anything that could be opposed, never being distinctive enough to provoke a reaction.

That's not a personality. That's absence of personality.

The things people dislike about you are often the same things that make you *you* — the strong opinions, the unusual interests, the refusal to conform on things that matter. Being too agreeable, too smooth, too universally palatable isn't a sign of a well-developed character. It's a sign that character has been sacrificed for comfort.

Put differently: if nobody dislikes anything about you, you probably haven't stood for anything. Some amount of being disliked is not a bug — it's evidence that you have edges, and edges are what give a personality its shape.

## The Reframe

When someone dislikes something about you, the instinct is to hear: *"There's something wrong with you."*

The logical reframe: *"This person has preferences, like every person does. For any trait I have, someone will dislike it. This tells me nothing about whether the trait is good or bad — it tells me that human preferences are diverse, which I already knew."*

This isn't about dismissing all feedback. Some criticism is useful — it points to things you actually want to change. The test is whether you want to change it *for yourself* or whether you want to change it to *eliminate someone's disapproval*. The first is growth. The second is chasing an impossibility.

## Related Topics to Explore

These frameworks address overlapping territory and are worth investigating further:

- **Stoicism** — The Stoics (Marcus Aurelius, Epictetus, Seneca) built an entire philosophy around distinguishing what's in your control from what isn't. Other people's opinions of you fall firmly in the "not in your control" category. The Stoic move is not to suppress the feeling but to recognize it's aimed at something you cannot influence.
- **Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)** — CBT formalizes the idea that emotions follow from beliefs, and irrational beliefs produce disproportionate emotional responses. The belief "everyone should like X about me" is a textbook cognitive distortion — specifically, an *overgeneralization* and an *unrealistic expectation*. CBT provides practical tools for identifying and restructuring these patterns.
- **Evolutionary Psychology** — The pain of social disapproval may be an evolved response from when group acceptance was literally a survival requirement. In small ancestral tribes, being disliked could mean being excluded, and exclusion meant death. The intensity of the feeling may be a vestige of that ancient pressure, firing in contexts (modern social life with billions of people) where it no longer serves its original purpose.
- **Philosophy of Preferences** — The structure of preferences across populations is studied in social choice theory and welfare economics. Arrow's Impossibility Theorem, while about group decision-making, captures a related insight: there is no way to aggregate diverse individual preferences into a single coherent ranking that satisfies everyone. Disapproval is structurally inevitable.
- **Existentialism** — Sartre's concept of "bad faith" (*mauvaise foi*) is relevant. Trying to become what others want you to be — rather than what you authentically are — is a form of self-deception. The authentic move is to own your traits and accept that others' reactions to them are their own.
