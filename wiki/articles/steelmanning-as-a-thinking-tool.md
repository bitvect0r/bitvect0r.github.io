# Steelmanning as a Thinking Tool

Steelmanning is the practice of constructing the strongest possible version of an argument one disagrees with — not the weakest, not a caricature, but the version that an intelligent proponent would recognise and endorse. Most arguments are against strawmen: simplified, distorted versions of opposing views that are easy to knock down. Steelmanning requires the opposite discipline: building up the opposition until it is genuinely hard to refute.

The test for a successful steelman is simple: could a person who actually holds the opposing view agree with the construction of their argument? If not, the exercise has failed — what was produced is a slightly better strawman, not a genuine engagement with the opposing position.

The value is not in proving the other side right. It is in discovering whether one's own position can withstand the best version of its opposition. If it cannot, then what was held was not a conviction — it was a habit of thought that had never been seriously tested. If it can, the conviction emerges stronger and better understood.

Critically, steelmanning one's own beliefs is at least as important as steelmanning opposing ones. Many people hold positions they have never subjected to serious challenge — positions inherited from upbringing, culture, or social environment. As [[the-scripts-we-inherit]] explores, the distinction between a genuine conviction and an inherited script is whether it has been tested against the strongest available counterargument. A belief that has never survived that test is not truly held — it is merely carried.

Steelmanning also functions as a defence against the intellectual self-righteousness described in [[the-mask-of-the-righteous]]. The righteous person never constructs the strongest argument against their own position. They assume correctness and build walls around the assumption. Steelmanning is the deliberate practice of breaching those walls to see whether the structure underneath can stand on its own. And as [[the-paradox-of-tolerance]] suggests, steelmanning even intolerant positions has practical value — it helps distinguish genuine threats to an open society from merely unpopular opinions that deserve protection.
