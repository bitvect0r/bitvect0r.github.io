# The Paradox of Tolerance

Karl Popper identified a structural flaw in unlimited tolerance: it is self-defeating. A society that tolerates everything — including those who seek to destroy tolerance — will eventually be overtaken by the intolerant. The tolerant are destroyed, and tolerance with them. Therefore, the preservation of tolerance requires a willingness to be intolerant of intolerance itself.

The logic is clean and uncomfortable. Openness to all ideas sounds like a virtue, but it includes openness to ideas whose explicit goal is the elimination of openness. If a society allows the intolerant to organise freely, recruit freely, and gain power through the very institutions that tolerance built, those institutions become the instruments of their own destruction.

**Tolerance must function as a contract, not a blanket principle.** Those who participate in tolerance receive its protections. Those who seek to dismantle it forfeit those protections. The system is not obligated to extend its benefits to people actively working to destroy it. But this framing immediately raises the hardest question: who decides where the line falls? What counts as intolerance dangerous enough to justify exclusion, versus merely unpopular opinion? Designing frameworks that can make this distinction without becoming tools of arbitrary suppression is the central challenge.

The argument has a personal dimension as well. What happens if a person is open to all ideas without any filter? They would absorb everything — including ideas designed to exploit that openness. Over time, the intolerant ideas would reshape them, and they would find themselves surrounded by people drawn to those ideas. Boundaries are not a failure of openness; they are its precondition. Maintaining tolerance requires active curation of what you allow to influence your thinking.

The Weimar Republic is the most cited historical case: a democratic system that tolerated the Nazi Party's use of democratic tools to dismantle democracy. But the mechanism described in [[freedom-movements-and-lost-liberty]] is equally relevant. Movements exploit the openness of free societies to accumulate power, then close that openness the moment they have enough of it. The tolerance that allowed them in is the first institution they dismantle.

Popper's argument is not about banning speech arbitrarily or suppressing unpopular opinions. It is about recognising that a liberal society has the right — and the obligation — to defend itself against forces that would use its freedoms as weapons against it. [[secularism-vs-ethno-nationalism]] extends this: a secular, tolerant society must actively resist ethno-nationalist movements that would use democratic tools to end democracy. Tolerance is not passive acceptance. It requires constant, difficult judgement.
