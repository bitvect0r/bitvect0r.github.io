# Persuasion vs Coercion in Mass Movements

We overestimate the power of persuasion. Propaganda alone has never built a mass movement. It does not convert the unconvinced — it confirms the already open, making them hear what they want to hear. Its true audience is not the outsider but the insider. **Propaganda exists to help us justify ourselves, not to convince others.** The more reason a movement's members have to feel guilty, the more fervent the propaganda becomes, because it is drowning out their own doubt, not answering anyone else's objections.

There is hardly an example in history of a mass movement reaching mass proportions through persuasion alone. Christianity, Islam, the great revolutions — they spread not through the power of ideas but through the power of armies. They converted people against their will, entered politics, and used institutional force. When given a choice between persuading and coercing, movements reliably choose coercion.

Both the willing convert and the forcefully converted require strong faith to survive the conversion. Without it, the willing convert is merely a proselytising terrorist, and the coerced convert is a coward who sold his soul. Both need to convince themselves that they are on the right side. This is where propaganda serves its actual function: it provides the internal narrative that makes participation bearable.

Violence and fanaticism form a reinforcing loop. When a true believer commits an act of violence, the act itself deepens his commitment to the cause — because he must now believe the cause was worth the violence. Every lynching in the American South did not merely intimidate Black communities; it also strengthened the white supremacist's belief in his own superiority. The coerced, counterintuitively, often become the most fanatical of all. As Hoffer puts it: "It takes fanatical faith to rationalise our cowardice."

The psychology of proselytising reveals something unexpected. The impulse to convert others is not evidence of strong faith — it is evidence of doubt. The proselytiser needs other people to validate his beliefs because he cannot fully validate them himself. The more people he can recruit, the more normal his position feels. This is a universal mechanism: controversial behaviour feels less controversial when more people participate. The creed whose legitimacy is most easily challenged develops the strongest proselytising impulse. Passion for proselytising and passion for world domination are, as Hoffer notes, both symptoms of a serious deficiency at the centre.

This connects to [[the-mask-of-the-righteous]]: the louder the propaganda, the deeper the guilt it is working to conceal. And to [[hatred-as-unifying-agent]]: coercion and hatred reinforce each other in cycles that escalate both the movement's violence and the depth of its members' commitment.
