# The Scripts We Inherit

People carry beliefs, behaviours, and identities that they never consciously chose. Some were installed by geography — the country, religion, and culture they were born into. Others were absorbed from family, community, and social environment. The question is whether a person can distinguish between a conviction they arrived at through deliberation and a script they inherited and never examined.

The difficulty is that a script running long enough becomes indistinguishable from a reasoned position. A belief held since childhood feels self-evident precisely because it was never challenged. The absence of examination feels like the presence of certainty. Someone raised in a particular political or religious tradition will hold positions that feel like conclusions but are actually starting conditions — premises they never chose, dressed up as discoveries they made.

Identity compounds the problem. People identify with their nationality, their religion, their political tribe. These identities are often facts — but facts can generate automatic programs. A person may oppose or support political movements, defend or attack ideologies, not because they reasoned their way to that position but because their identity scripts demand it. The script says: people like me believe X. And so they believe X.

The practical test for distinguishing conviction from script is [[steelmanning-as-a-thinking-tool]]. If someone cannot construct the strongest possible argument for the positions they oppose, they likely do not understand those positions well enough to genuinely disagree. A real conviction survives contact with the best version of its opposition. A script collapses the moment it encounters an argument it was never designed to handle. The inability to steelman is diagnostic — it reveals where thought ends and inheritance begins.

This connects to what [[the-mask-of-the-righteous]] describes from the outside: the person certain of their righteousness without having tested it. The question of inherited scripts asks the same thing from the inside. The willingness to ask — honestly, without a reassuring answer already prepared — may be the only defence against becoming the person who mistakes inherited certainty for earned wisdom.

Scripts extend beyond belief. Visual vocabularies are inherited the same way: the sunset silhouette, the plate-of-food from directly above, the pointing-at-the-monument pose, absorbed unconsciously from a camera roll and mistaken for personal taste. [[photographing-a-trip]] treats this as a species of the same pattern — an aesthetic inheritance that returns a trip indistinguishable from everyone else's unless examined.
