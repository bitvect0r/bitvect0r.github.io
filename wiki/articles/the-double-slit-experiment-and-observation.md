# The Double Slit Experiment and Observation

Fire electrons at a barrier with two slits. If no one observes which slit each electron passes through, an interference pattern appears on the detector — the signature of wave-like behaviour, as if each electron somehow passed through both slits simultaneously.

Now add a way to observe the electrons' paths. Shine photons with a wavelength short enough to determine which slit each electron used. The interference pattern vanishes. The probabilities from the two slits add up normally, as if the electrons were ordinary particles following definite paths.

The transition between the two behaviours is not abrupt — it depends on the precision of the observation. If the photons' wavelength is longer than the distance between the slits, the observation is too coarse to determine the path. In that case, interference returns. The pattern correlates precisely with how much information about the path is available.

**The rule is stark: if which-path information exists, there is no interference. If it does not exist, interference appears.** The universe's behaviour changes depending on whether information about the electron's path is available — not on whether anyone looks at that information, but on whether it exists at all.

This is deeply unlike anything in everyday experience. Watching a ball pass through a doorway does not change its trajectory. Observation, in the macroscopic world, is passive. At the quantum level, the relationship between information and physical behaviour is fundamental. The presence or absence of which-path information is not a side effect of measurement — it is entangled with the physical outcome. Reality, at its most basic level, appears to be structured around what can and cannot be known about it.
