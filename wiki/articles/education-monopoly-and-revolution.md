# Education Monopoly and Revolution

Revolution requires language before it requires action. Before anyone can organise against a regime, someone must articulate what is wrong with it — name the injustice in terms that make people feel it. Hoffer calls these people "men of words": intellectuals, writers, critics. They are the precondition for any mass movement.

The problem is structural: **when education is monopolised by the ruling class, the only people capable of articulating dissent are those invested in the system.** Every man of words is affiliated with power. They tolerate its abuses because their status, livelihood, and identity depend on it. The regime does not need to censor them — they censor themselves, because dissent would mean dismantling the foundation they stand on.

The 10th-century Catholic Church illustrates this perfectly. Corruption was rampant, but all educated people were clergy. Learning was a monopoly of the institution that most needed to be criticised. Church-affiliated scholars tolerated the abuses because they had nowhere else to stand. It was the printing press in the 15th century that broke the monopoly. Suddenly, non-clerical educated people appeared — thinkers with no institutional loyalty to protect and no reason to stay quiet. The Reformation was not primarily a theological event; it was the consequence of literacy escaping the institution that had contained it.

India's caste system survived for centuries through the same mechanism. Education was restricted to the higher castes. The lower castes lacked not just rights but the literacy that produces the vocabulary of rights. It was during British rule, when colonial institutions gave lower-caste communities access to education, that they developed both the language and the organisational capacity to fight back. Before that, every man of words was structurally aligned with the rulers.

Hitler understood this with strategic clarity. His plan to restrict learning to the elite and keep the masses illiterate was not arbitrary cruelty — it was the logical response to the mechanism described here. A literate population is a political population. The fastest way to prevent revolution is to prevent the reading that makes revolution thinkable.

Foreign influence plays a recurring and ironic role. Colonial powers build educational institutions to produce administrators loyal to the empire. Instead, they produce intellectuals with no loyalty to the empire. The British built schools in India to serve their colonial project; those schools created the independence leaders who dismantled it. Every colonial education system plants the seeds of its own undoing, because education, once distributed, cannot be directed.

The implication is straightforward: **education must be decentralised across every class, community, and social group in a society.** But this immediately runs into [[why-the-abjectly-poor-never-revolt]]: the people most excluded from education are also the people most excluded from everything else. Those without food do not think about literacy. Their social class is so far from education that it does not register as a possibility. The abjectly poor lack not just material resources but the literacy that would let them name what they lack.
