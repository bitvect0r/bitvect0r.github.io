# Hatred as Unifying Agent

The strength of a mass movement is proportional to the vividness of its devil. The sharper the enemy is drawn, the more powerful the movement becomes. Of all the forces available to a mass movement, hatred is the most efficient unifier — it requires no ideology, no plan, no intellectual framework. It only requires a target.

The pattern recurs across every political context. Nationalist movements cast religious minorities as infiltrators. Separatist movements cast majority communities as oppressors. The ideological content differs; the structural function is identical. Define the enemy clearly, and the movement assembles itself around the shared hostility. People who agree on nothing else will unite over a common hatred. Incompatible personalities become allies the moment they share a devil.

The **ideal devil is a foreigner** — or someone who can be made to look like one. Communities that have lived on the same land for centuries can be recast as outsiders through the right framing. Religious identity, ethnic background, or historical migration patterns become the raw material for manufacturing foreignness, regardless of how deep the community's actual roots are.

Hoffer makes a counterintuitive claim about where hatred originates: **it stems from self-contempt.** A person who feels genuinely secure does not hate those they consider beneath them. Hatred is reserved for those who trigger a suspicion of one's own inadequacy — people who seem stronger, more successful, or more legitimate. When people are wronged, their anger often lands not on the actual perpetrator but on a more convenient target. The hatred is real; its direction is arbitrary.

> "Self-righteousness is a loud din raised to drown the voice of guilt within us."

Shame does not produce humility. Confronting people with their guilt sharpens their aggression, because the aggression was always there to protect against exactly that confrontation. People silence their guilty conscience by convincing themselves that those they have harmed deserve everything they got. And this reveals a deeper mechanism: **perceiving people as a group rather than as individuals erodes the sense of their humanity.** When a community becomes *that group* in the mind — a category rather than a collection of individual lives — empathy collapses.

One of Hoffer's most striking observations is that hatred produces imitation. People gradually become what they hate. The admiration hidden inside hostility pulls them toward the enemy's methods, rhetoric, and posture. Movements opposing authoritarian regimes adopt authoritarian tactics. Groups fighting dehumanisation begin dehumanising. The hatred is convenient for building unity, but its long-term cost is the corruption of the values that justified the movement in the first place. This is one of the mechanisms through which [[freedom-movements-and-lost-liberty]] describes movements betraying their own ideals.

Passionate hatred can fill an empty life. For someone with no purpose and no identity they can stand behind, hating a group becomes the organising principle of existence. This is the same psychological vacuum that [[true-believer-vs-true-revolutionary]] describes — the true believer needs a cause, and hatred provides the most accessible one available.
