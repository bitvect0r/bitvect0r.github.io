# The Dictator's Treadmill

Dictatorship is a treadmill that never stops. Getting on is easy. Getting off without being destroyed is nearly impossible. And you cannot get back on once you step off.

The incentive to keep running is obvious — power and money flow easily to a dictator. But the real trap is not greed. It is accumulated liability. Every year in power creates new enemies and new crimes that require the apparatus of power for protection. The dictator does not stay because he wants to. He stays because stopping would expose him to everything he has done.

Every exit is worse than continuing. Replacing yourself with a loyal subordinate requires someone competent enough to hold power — but a competent successor can turn against you, as Nazarbayev discovered. Transitioning to democracy means surrendering power, money, and immunity from prosecution, and your entire inner circle faces the same exposure. Exile grows riskier as international institutions like the ICC narrow the list of safe harbours. The rational calculation, in almost every scenario, favours staying on the treadmill.

**Tyrants rarely choose democracy. They fall into it.** The historical record is not one of strongmen nobly transitioning to elections. It is one of miscalculation, death, loss of control, and democratic systems filling the resulting vacuum. Voluntary transitions are vanishingly rare because the arithmetic almost never works: surrender everything you have for a probability — not a guarantee — of not being prosecuted.

The Golden Gun Paradox captures the fragility at the heart of this system. **At the point where a tyrant must deploy force to save himself, it is already too late.** Power works best when its use is implicit — when everyone understands what would happen if they stepped out of line, without that understanding ever being tested. The moment coercion becomes visible, it reveals weakness rather than strength. Legitimacy evaporates under open brutality in a way it does not under the mere threat of it. The show of force is not a demonstration of power but a confession that power is slipping.

As [[submissive-societies-and-mass-movements]] explores, dictators survive longest in societies with a deep tradition of submission. In societies with a tradition of freedom, any improvement in economic conditions reactivates the cultural memory of revolt, and the treadmill becomes unsustainable.
